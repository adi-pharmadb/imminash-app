0.1  What is ACS and Why Does This Tool Exist?
The following is a plain-English explanation of the Australian immigration process this tool supports. Understanding this context is essential for building the product correctly.


ESSENTIAL CONTEXT — READ BEFORE BUILDING
THE IMMIGRATION CONTEXT — plain English


Australia allows skilled workers to migrate permanently through a points-based visa system called the General Skilled Migration (GSM) program. To apply, a person must first prove their skills are genuine by getting a Skills Assessment from an approved body.


For ICT (Information and Communication Technology) workers, that approved body is the ACS — the Australian Computer Society. ACS is a professional membership organisation that has been delegated authority by the Australian government to assess ICT migrants.


The ACS assessment process works like this:
1. The applicant nominates an occupation code (called an ANZSCO code) that matches their work
2. ACS reviews their qualifications and employment history
3. ACS decides if their skills meet Australian ICT standards for that occupation
4. If approved, ACS issues a result letter the applicant uses in their visa application


The assessment costs AUD $1,498 and takes 8–12 weeks. Applications are frequently refused — the most common reason is poorly written employment reference letters that do not adequately describe the applicant's duties.


This is the problem imminash solves. The tool guides migrants through producing high-quality, ACS-compliant documents before they submit, reducing the risk of refusal.
	

0.2  What Happens if Documents Are Poor Quality?
This is critical for understanding why the AI conversation design in Section 03 and 04 must be built exactly as specified.


If documents are good quality
	If documents are poor quality
	• ACS approves the assessment
• Applicant receives a result letter
• Applicant can lodge their visa application

	• ACS refuses the assessment
• Applicant must reapply and pay $1,498 again

This is why the AI must ask specific, probing questions and refuse to generate documents from vague answers. The document quality directly determines whether our users succeed or fail — and whether imminash has a business.


0.3  Glossary — Every Technical Term Used in This Document
Every term below is used throughout this brief. If you encounter a term you don't recognise, look it up here first.


Term
	Plain English definition
	ACS
	Australian Computer Society. The government-approved body that assesses ICT migrants' skills for visa purposes.
	ANZSCO
	Australia and New Zealand Standard Classification of Occupations. A numbered code system that categorises jobs. Example: ICT Business Analyst = 261111. The applicant must nominate one code that matches their work.
	Skills Assessment
	The formal process where ACS reviews qualifications and work history to determine if someone meets Australian ICT standards. Costs AUD $1,498.
	GSM
	General Skilled Migration. The points-based visa program through which skilled workers apply for permanent residency in Australia.
	PR
	Permanent Residency. The goal of most imminash users.
	SFIA
	Skills Framework for the Information Age. An international framework that describes ICT job roles at different skill levels. ACS uses SFIA to evaluate whether an applicant's duties match their nominated occupation.
	BUSA
	Business Analysis — a specific SFIA skill code. ACS assessors check whether an ICT Business Analyst applicant's duties demonstrate BUSA-level work.
	BPRE
	Business Process Re-Engineering — another SFIA skill. Relevant to ICT Business Analyst and similar occupations.
	Employment Reference Letter
	A letter written on company letterhead by the employer, describing the applicant's job title, employment dates, hours, and duties. This is the most critical document in an ACS application.
	Supporting Statement
	A document written in the first person by the applicant (or on their behalf) explaining how their qualifications and experience meet the nominated occupation's requirements.
	CPD
	Continuing Professional Development. A record of training, courses, certifications, or professional activities completed to maintain and develop skills. ACS may ask for this.
	RPL
	Recognition of Prior Learning. An alternative ACS pathway for applicants with no formal ICT qualification but extensive work experience (6+ years). Requires project reports instead of qualification documents.
	MLTSSL
	Medium and Long-term Strategic Skills List. A government list of occupations eligible for certain visa types. Whether an occupation is on this list affects which visas the applicant can apply for.
	STSOL
	Short-term Skilled Occupation List. Another government occupations list with different visa eligibility rules.
	189 Visa
	Skilled Independent visa. Permanent residency without needing employer or state sponsorship. Requires ACS approval and sufficient points.
	190 Visa
	Skilled Nominated visa. Permanent residency with state/territory nomination. Requires ACS approval and state nomination.
	491 Visa
	Skilled Work Regional visa. Temporary visa (5 years) for regional areas. Can lead to permanent residency.
	BRD
	Business Requirements Document. A formal document describing what a business needs from an IT system or project. Producing BRDs is evidence of ICT Business Analyst work.
	FSD
	Functional Specification Document. A technical document describing how a system will work. Relevant to ICT BA duties.
	Duty statement
	A sentence or paragraph describing a specific work responsibility. ACS assesses duty statements to determine if the work was at the right ICT skill level.
	Host company
	When a worker is placed by a recruitment agency, the host company is the business where they actually worked. ACS requires reference letters from the host company, not the agency.
	Supabase
	The backend-as-a-service platform currently used for authentication (login) in the imminash product.
	OAuth
	Open Authorisation. A standard protocol for sign-in via third-party accounts (e.g. Google Sign-In). Recommended replacement for magic link in this product.

Screen-by-screen specification of every layout state
	

2.1  The Three Zones — Always, Default, and Triggered
The workspace has three zones. Zone 1 is always visible. Zone 2 is the default state. Zone 3 only appears when triggered by the AI. This is the same pattern used by ChatGPT, Claude, and Notion — users understand it immediately.


SCREEN STATE 1 — DEFAULT (USER ARRIVES, CONVERSATION IN PROGRESS)
	Left: Zone 1 — Navigation bar  |  ~220px wide  |  Fixed, does not scroll
Contents from top to bottom:
* imminash logo (top left)
* Occupation badge: '[Occupation Name] | ANZSCO [code] | [Assessing Body]' — e.g. 'ICT Business Analyst | ANZSCO 261111 | ACS'
* Divider line
* Document list — see Section 2.2 for detail
* Bottom: progress indicator — e.g. '0 of 4 documents complete'


Right: Zone 2 — Full-width chat  |  Fills all remaining screen width
Contents:
* Chat history — scrollable, messages from top
* Fixed chat input bar at bottom — text field + send button + CV upload icon
	SCREEN STATE 2 — SPLIT VIEW (AI HAS GENERATED A DOCUMENT FOR REVIEW)
Trigger: AI sends a message saying 'I've drafted your [document name] — review it on the right.' The screen splits at this moment.
Left half (50% of Zone 2): Chat continues. The most recent AI message is visible. Input bar remains active.
Right half (50% of Zone 2 = Zone 3): Document preview panel appears. Contains:
* Document title at top (e.g. 'Employment Reference Letter — Accenture')
* Scrollable document preview — formatted to look like the final output (letterhead style, correct font, paragraph structure)
* Two fixed buttons at bottom: 'Approve this document' (green) and 'Request changes' (outlined/secondary)
	SCREEN STATE 3 — DOCUMENT APPROVED (SPLIT COLLAPSES, CONVERSATION RESUMES)
Trigger: User clicks 'Approve this document'.
Zone 3 closes with a smooth animation. Chat returns to full width. The approved document's dot in Zone 1 turns green. The progress indicator updates.
AI sends next message beginning the next document's information gathering.
	SCREEN STATE 4 — ALL COMPLETE (DOWNLOAD SCREEN)
Trigger: All documents approved. All dots green.
Chat area shows a completion message from the AI summarising what was produced and what the user needs to do next (get employer signatures, submit to ACS).
Zone 1 bottom: 'Download Package' button activates — prominent, blue, full width of Zone 1. Zone 2: agent review offer appears below the AI completion message.
	

MOBILE BEHAVIOUR


* Zone 1 collapses to a hamburger icon in the top left. Tapping opens a slide-out drawer.
* Zone 2 (chat) is full width on mobile — no split state.
* When a document is ready for review on mobile: AI message contains a 'View document' button. Tapping opens the document in a full-screen modal with Approve and Request Changes buttons at the bottom.
* After approving on mobile, modal closes and chat resumes.


ACCEPTANCE CRITERIA
• All 4 screen states render correctly on desktop (1280px+ width)
• Mobile layout follows the mobile behaviour rules above
• Zone 1 is never hidden on desktop — it is always visible
• Split animation is smooth — no jarring layout shift
• Document preview is scrollable independently of the chat
• 'Approve' button turns the correct document's dot green and collapses Zone 3
• 'Request changes' refocuses the chat input with placeholder text: 'Describe what you'd like to change...'
	

Priority: P0  |  Effort: L  |  Owner: Frontend

AI BEHAVIOUR RULES
AI behaviour rules — these apply to every step:
• Never ask two unrelated questions in the same message
• Never re-ask something already answered
• If an answer is vague, ask a follow-up before proceeding — do not generate from vague input
• If user says they don't know, explain why it matters and suggest how to find out
• Tone: professional, warm, direct. Not robotic. Not overly casual. This is a high-stakes process.
• If user seems frustrated or overwhelmed, acknowledge it: 'I know this is detailed — it's important because ACS assessors specifically look for this level of specificity.'
	

3.3  CPD Log — Detail
CPD stands for Continuing Professional Development. It is a record of activities that demonstrate the applicant keeps their ICT skills current. ACS may request this as part of the assessment. The tool generates it proactively so the user has it ready.

* Online courses or certifications completed (e.g. AWS, Google Cloud, Microsoft, Coursera, LinkedIn Learning, Udemy)

* Workshops or training programmes attended through an employer

3.4  Document Checklist — What It Must Contain
The document checklist is the final document in the package. It tells the user everything they need to submit to ACS, in the order they need to submit it. It is personalised based on their occupation, qualification level, and number of employers.


CHECKLIST SECTIONS — ALL MUST BE PRESENT


* Identity documents: passport bio page, any name change evidence
* Qualification documents: degree certificate, official academic transcripts (for each qualification)
* English translations: if any documents are not in English, certified translations required
* Employment evidence (for each employer): reference letter on official letterhead signed by authorised signatory, plus one form of supporting evidence per employer (payslips, tax records, superannuation statements, or bank statements showing salary deposits)
* CV/Resume: tailored to ACS requirements
* CPD Log: the document generated in this tool
* Supporting Statement: the document generated in this tool
* ACS application fee: AUD $1,498 — payable at time of submission at acs.org.au
* Submission format: all documents combined into a single PDF, maximum 3MB, not encrypted or read-only


CRITICAL REMINDER — MUST APPEAR ON CHECKLIST
The checklist must include a prominent reminder about employer signatures: 'Your reference letters must be printed on each employer's official company letterhead and signed by an authorised person (manager, HR, or director) with their name, title, and contact details. imminash has prepared the content — your employer provides the letterhead and signature.'

3.6  Edge Cases — Required AI Behaviour
The following situations will occur in real usage. The AI must handle all of them. Ignoring these will result in poor user experience or incorrect documents.


Edge case
	Required AI response
	User has no paid ICT experience
	AI must say: 'ACS requires paid employment of at least 20 hours per week. Without this, your application may not be eligible under the General Skills pathway. However, if you have 6+ years of ICT experience, the RPL pathway may be available to you. Would you like to discuss this?' Do not proceed with document generation for ineligible applications.
	User worked part-time (under 20hrs/week)
	AI must flag: 'ACS will not count employment under 20 hours per week — this period may be assessed as ineligible. I'll include it in your letter but flag this risk clearly. You may want to focus on your full-time experience.'
	User worked via a recruitment agency
	AI must note: 'For agency placements, ACS requires the reference letter to come from the host company (the business where you actually worked), not the agency. Make sure you obtain the letter from [host company name], not [agency name].'
	Employment overlaps (two jobs at same time)
	AI must explain: 'ACS will only count one period of employment for any overlapping timeframe. For the period [date] to [date] where both roles overlap, only the one more closely aligned to ICT Business Analyst duties will be counted toward your total years.'
	Graduation date is after employment start
	AI must flag: 'ACS generally requires work experience to be post-qualification for the General Skills pathway. Your role at [employer] appears to have started before you graduated. This period may be assessed differently — I'll flag this in your supporting statement.'
	User abandons mid-conversation (returns later)
	All conversation data must be saved to the user's account. When they return, the AI resumes from where it left off: 'Welcome back, [name]. We were working on your reference letter for [employer]. Let's pick up where we left off.'
	User says they can't remember specific details
	AI responds: 'That's okay — let's try to reconstruct it. Do you have any old performance reviews, project documents, or emails that might help? If not, give me your best recollection and I can help frame it appropriately. Even rough details are better than nothing.'
	User asks a question not related to the document
	AI responds helpfully but redirects: 'That's a good question — [brief answer]. For now, let's stay focused on getting your documents done. Back to [current step]...'

04
	ACS Assessment Criteria
Eligibility rules, duty requirements, elicitation questions, and AI system prompt skeleton
	

4.1  ACS General Eligibility Rules
The AI must know and apply these rules. When a user's situation violates a rule, the AI must flag it immediately and explain the consequence — not proceed silently.


Rule
	The requirement
	What AI must say if violated
	Minimum work hours
	Must be 20+ hours per week. ACS will reject employment under this threshold.
	'ACS requires at least 20 hours per week. This period may be assessed as ineligible. I'll flag this in your documents.'
	Paid employment only
	Volunteering, thesis research, and unpaid internships do not count.
	'ACS only counts paid work. This period cannot be included in your application.'
	Post-qualification experience
	For General Skills pathway, experience should be after your qualification's completion date.
	'This experience appears to predate your qualification. ACS may not count it toward your total years.'
	Experience within 8 years
	Only experience from the last 8 years counts toward the assessment.
	'ACS typically only counts experience within the past 8 years. Experience before [8 years ago date] may not be counted.'
	Overlapping employment
	If two jobs overlap, only one period counts. ACS counts the most relevant one.
	'These roles overlap between [dates]. ACS will only count one period — I'll use the role more closely aligned to your nominated occupation.'
	Agency roles — host company letter
	Reference letter must come from the host company, not the recruitment agency.
	'For this role, ACS requires the letter from [host company], not [agency]. Please obtain the letter directly from your workplace.'
	RPL — experience within 2 years
	For RPL pathway, most recent ICT experience must be within the last 2 years.
	'ACS requires your most recent experience to be within the last 2 years for the RPL pathway. Your most recent role appears to be more than 2 years ago — this may affect eligibility.'
	

4.2  Duty Elicitation Questions — ICT Business Analyst (261111)
These 8 questions must be asked for every employer. They are occupation-specific and mapped to ACS/SFIA assessment criteria. Different ANZSCO codes require different questions — this section covers 261111 only. When other occupations are added, a similar question set must be developed for each.


WHY THESE QUESTIONS — FOR THE CTO
Why these specific questions: ACS assessors use the SFIA (Skills Framework for the Information Age) to evaluate whether an applicant's duties match their nominated occupation. For ICT Business Analyst (261111), the key SFIA skills are Business Analysis (BUSA), Business Process Re-Engineering (BPRE), Requirements Engineering, and Stakeholder Relationship Management. Each question below is designed to elicit evidence of one or more of these skills.

5.1  CV Upload — Behaviour Specification
CV upload is offered first, before any questions. It is never required. The tool must produce identical-quality output whether or not a CV is uploaded. The CV is used only to skip questions the AI can answer from the CV — it never replaces the duty elicitation questions.


What CV upload extracts
	What CV upload never replaces
	• Employer names, job titles, employment dates
• Full-time vs part-time indicators
• Qualification names, institutions, graduation years
• Certifications and professional memberships
• Potential ACS eligibility flags (e.g. overlapping dates, early career)
	• The 8 ACS duty elicitation questions — always asked
• The conversation itself — CV does not skip the chat
• Vague CV duty descriptions — these are never used verbatim in output documents
• The supporting statement — always generated from conversation content
	

CV quality handling — three scenarios


CV quality
	What AI does
	Example AI message
	Good CV (detailed, specific ICT duties)
	Extract all structured data. Confirm with user. Still ask all 8 duty questions per employer — do not assume CV duties are ACS-compliant.
	'Great CV — I've found 3 roles and your Bachelor's degree. I'll use this as a starting point. I still need to ask you some specific questions about each role so the letters meet ACS standards.'
	Average CV (basic job descriptions, dates present)
	Extract structured data (dates, titles, employers). Flag that duty descriptions are not detailed enough for ACS. Ask all 8 duty questions using CV as context.
	'I've extracted your job history. Your CV descriptions are a good start but ACS needs more detail — let's work through each role together to build stronger duty statements.'
	Poor CV (vague, irrelevant, or outdated)
	Extract what is possible. Inform user clearly. Proceed with full question set — do not let poor CV quality degrade the conversation.
	'Your CV doesn't give me enough detail for ACS requirements — that's fine, it's common. Your answers to my questions matter more than your CV. Let's go through each role together.'

Agent review — what the Studynash agent actually checks
This is what the reviewing agent is expected to evaluate. This checklist should be provided to the agent along with the documents.


* Do the duty statements in each reference letter align with the ANZSCO occupation description for the nominated code?
* Are duties specific enough — do they describe concrete tasks, outputs, and outcomes, or are they still vague?
* Does each letter state hours per week (20+) and confirm paid employment?
* Does the supporting statement coherently connect qualifications and experience to the nominated occupation?
* Are there any ACS eligibility issues that have not been flagged (overlapping dates, pre-qualification experience, agency roles)?
* Is the overall application narrative internally consistent — do the CV, letters, and supporting statement all tell the same story?


Agent review — Studynash workflow


* When user purchases agent review: system creates a task in Studynash CRM (Odoo) with: user name, occupation, assessing body, document package attached, and a 2-business-day due date
* Agent receives email notification with task link
* Agent opens documents, reviews against the checklist above, adds comments in track-changes mode (Word documents)
* Agent marks task complete in Odoo. System sends user an email with revised documents attached, or an approval confirmation if no changes needed
* User downloads revised documents from the email link
